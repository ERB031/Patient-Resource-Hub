SSI Eligibility

You are eligible for SSI if you:

  • are 65+ years old, blind1, or disabled2
  • have limited resources & income3
  • are a U.S. citizen or a qualified alien
  • are a resident of one of the 50 states, the District of Columbia, or Northern Mariana Islands4
  • are not absent from the country for more than 30 days
  • are not confined to an institution (such as a hospital or prison) at the government’s expense5
  • have applied for all other cash benefits you may be eligible for (such as pensions or Social Security)

If you appear to meet these requirements, your case will move on to the disability determination process.

1 Blindness is defined as having central visual acuity for distance of 20/200 or less in your better eye with use of a correcting lens, or having a visual field limitation in your better eye such that the widest diameter of the visual field subtends an angle no greater than 20 degrees.

2 An adult is considered disabled if they have a medically determinable physical or mental impairment expected to last over 12 months (or result in death) that prevents any substantial gainful activity. Substantial gainful activity includes full- or part-time work with earnings averaging over $1,310 per month for 2021.

3 Limited resources (as defined by SSA) means you have less than $2,000 in assets if you are an individual or $3,000 if you are a couple. Assets include cash, bank accounts, land, vehicles, personal property, life insurance, or anything that can be converted to cash. SSA does not count your home, the lot it sits on, household goods, life insurance up to $1,500 face value, one car (in most cases), a burial plot, or burial funds up to $1,500. Learn more about resource requirements and income thresholds for SSI.

4 Children of military personnel whose parent(s) are assigned to permanent duty ashore anywhere outside the United States may continue to get SSI benefits. Students temporarily studying abroad as part of an educational program may also continue SSI payments.

5 If you are incarcerated, you may be able to apply for prerelease benefits. Learn more about how incarceration affects benefits here .
