ABLE Accounts

Save for disability goals without losing essential benefits.

An ABLE Account is a tax-advantaged savings account that allows individuals with disabilities to build savings without affecting their eligibility for SSI, Medicaid, and other means-tested programs (FAFSA, HUD, SNAP, etc.). Investment income grows tax-free, and anyone can contribute—including friends, caregivers, and family.

The first $100,000 in an ABLE account do not count toward SSI’s resource limit. If your balance climbs higher, SSI payments pause, but Medicaid continues; once the balance falls below $100,000, SSI restarts without a new application.

Eligibility

You are eligible to open an ABLE account if:

  1. You have a disability with onset prior to your 26th birthday 1.
  2. You meet the severity of disability requirement by:
    1. Receiving SSI or SSDI.
    2. Having your doctor complete a disability certification affirming “marked and severe” functional limitations that are expected to last at least 12 months or result in death 2.

You cannot have more than one ABLE account.

Not all states run ABLE programs, and some offer extra perks like tax deductions or ABLE-linked debit cards. Compare current state programs to see what is available or accepting out-of-state applicants.

You can open an account in any state program as long as you meet its eligibility criteria and the program is open to out-of-state enrollment.

If your disability began before age 26, but SSA recorded an onset date after age 26 for SSI or SSDI, you can still pursue an ABLE account with a physician certification confirming when your disability actually began and that it meets SSA’s definition of significant functional limitations. SSA staff are not responsible for determining ABLE onset dates.

Congress is considering legislation to raise the age-of-onset cutoff to 46. Support the ABLE Age Adjustment Act if it would help you or your community.

1 You do not have to be under age 26 today—your disability simply needs to have started before your 26th birthday.

2 If you no longer meet disability criteria, your ABLE account stays open until year-end. After that, new contributions stop and withdrawals count as non-qualified, which means they are taxable and may affect benefits.

Contributions

Any person 1 can contribute to an ABLE account, including friends, family, and community members.

Contributions must be made with post-tax dollars. They are not federally tax deductible, though some states offer deductions or credits for their programs.

Contribution limits

  • Combined deposits from all contributors (you, family, friends, etc.) are capped at $15,000 per tax year.
  • Lifetime caps depend on the state program and currently range from $235,000 to $589,000.

Most programs offer multiple investment portfolios and allow two changes per year to your investment mix.

1 The IRS defines a “person” as an individual, trust, estate, partnership, association, company, or corporation—not just a single human.

Spending

ABLE funds must be used for qualified disability expenses (QDEs).

QDEs cover essentials like education, food, housing, transportation, employment training and support, assistive technology, personal support services, healthcare costs, financial management, administrative services, and other expenses that improve health, independence, or quality of life.

Review the ABLE National Resource Center QDE guidance for more detail.

Some state programs offer debit cards for easier spending. Otherwise you can transfer funds to a checking account, though a holding period may apply.

Once money leaves the ABLE account and sits in a checking account, it can count toward asset limits—avoid keeping large transfers unspent for too long.

Employment

ABLE accounts increase allowable savings; they do not change how SSA treats earned income. Wages continue to affect benefits the same way, even if you move that income into an ABLE account.

The ABLE to Work provision lets employed account holders exceed the $15,000 annual contribution limit. You can deposit an additional $12,760 1 (or your gross income for the year, whichever is lower).

1 The extra allowance is $15,950 in Alaska and $14,680 in Hawaii.

College

The FAFSA application excludes ABLE balances from the assets it counts.

You can roll over money from a 529 college savings plan into an ABLE account without taxes or penalties as long as both accounts are held by you (or a qualified family member) and the rollover stays within the annual ABLE contribution limit.
