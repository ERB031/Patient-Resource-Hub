Income Requirements

Understand how the Social Security Administration (SSA) counts earned, unearned, in-kind, and deemed income for SSI and how SSDI treats work activity.

Back to disability benefits

How SSA defines income

SSA considers any item you receive in cash or in kind that can be used to meet your need for food or shelter to be "income."

Types of income SSA reviews

  • Earned income. Includes wages, net earnings (income minus expenses) from self-employment, certain royalties, honoraria, and sheltered workshop payments.
  • In-kind income. Includes food or shelter that you get for free or for less than its fair market value.1
  • Unearned income. Includes Social Security benefits, pensions, state disability payments, unemployment benefits, interest income, dividends, and cash from friends and relatives.
  • Deemed income.2 SSA may count part of the income of a spouse or parent you live with when deciding whether you qualify for SSI.

Generally, the more countable income you have, the less SSI benefits you will receive. If your countable income exceeds the allowable limit, you cannot receive SSI.

To determine how your income affects your payment, SSA subtracts any income that does not count (see the list below) from your total income. The remaining amount is your "countable income," which is deducted from the SSI federal benefit rate to get your monthly SSI benefit. See example calculations from SSA.

SSDI is only affected by your earned income. If you perform work that is over the substantial gainful activity (SGA) limit, you are not eligible for benefits unless you are in a Trial Work Period as part of the SSDI work incentives program.

Income that does not count when calculating SSI benefits

SSA excludes several income sources when figuring your payment, including:

  • Your first $20 of income per month.
  • Your first $65 of earnings per month and one-half of any additional earnings over $65.
  • SNAP/Food Stamps benefits.
  • Income tax refunds.
  • Home energy assistance.
  • Need-based assistance that is funded by a state, local government, or tribal program.
  • Small amounts of income received irregularly or infrequently.
  • Grants, scholarships, fellowships, or gifts used for tuition or educational expenses.
  • Food or shelter based on need provided by nonprofit agencies.
  • Income earned in the first nine months of a Trial Work Period (SSDI).
  • Impairment-Related Work Expenses .
  • Loans that you must repay.
  • Money someone else spends to pay your expenses for items other than food or shelter (for example, telephone or medical bills).
  • Disaster assistance.
  • Your first $2,000 of compensation received for participating in a clinical trial.
  • Refundable federal and advanced tax credits received on or after January 1, 2010.
  • Certain exclusions on Indian trust fund payments.
  • Income set aside under a Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS) .
  • Earnings up to $1,930 per month (maximum $7,770 per year) for a student under age 22 using the Student Earned Income Exclusion (SSI).

1 In-kind support and maintenance is food, shelter, or both that somebody else provides for you. Receiving this support can reduce your monthly SSI benefits by as much as $284.66 depending on the value of the help you receive. Learn more and see example calculations from SSA here .

2 Deemed income no longer applies if you do not live with your spouse or parent, when you turn 18 (if receiving child benefits), or when your alien sponsorship ends.
