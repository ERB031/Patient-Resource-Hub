SSI Eligibility for Children

To be eligible for SSI, a child must:

  • be unmarried and not the head of a household
  • under age 18 (or under age 22 and a full-time student) - there is no minimum age requirement.
  • have limited income & resources1
  • be blind OR have a medically determinable physical or mental impairment resulting in marked and severe functional limitations that is suspecting to last 12 months or more or result in death.

1 SSA may consider a portion of the parents' (or adoptive/stepparents') income and resources as if they were available to the child, including children who are temporarily away at school if they return home during weekends, holidays, or summer vacation and remain subject to parental control. This process is called "deeming parental income and resources."

Generally, if a child under age 18 lives with one parent, $2,000 of the parent's total countable resources does not count. If the child lives with 2 parents, $3,000 does not count. SSA count amounts over the parents' limits as part of the child's $2,000 resource limit. See the "deeming eligibility chart" for more info.

Income/resources that does not count toward the "deeming" process:

  • income used to make court-ordered support payments
  • the home that the beneficiary resides in
  • one vehicle that is used for transportation
  • money in pension funds.
  • Public Income Maintenance payments (PIM) such as Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF)
  • certain Veterans Affairs' pensions
  • general assistance
  • foster care payments for an ineligible child

Deeming from the parent stops when a child reaches age 18, gets married, or no longer lives with a parent. Deeming does not apply if:

  • the disabled child receives a reduced SSI benefit in a medical treatment facility
  • the child is eligible for Medicaid under a State home care plan
  • deeming would otherwise cause ineligibility for SSI benefits
