Medicaid

Medicaid provides free or low-cost health coverage to millions of Americans, including some low-income people, families and children, pregnant women, the elderly, and people with disabilities.

States administer their own Medicaid programs according to federal requirements, meaning that eligibility, costs, and coverage vary between states. For General information about Medicaid, see the links below:

Medicaid Eligibility

Medicaid eligibility varies by state but generally includes:

  • Low-income adults, children, and pregnant women
  • Elderly adults and people with disabilities
  • People who need long-term care

States that expanded Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act cover adults under 65 with incomes up to 138% of the federal poverty level.

How to Apply for Medicaid

You can apply for Medicaid several ways:

  • Through the Health Insurance Marketplace at HealthCare.gov
  • Directly through your state Medicaid agency
  • By contacting your local Medicaid office

The application process requires documentation of your income, household size, citizenship or immigration status, and other qualifying factors.

Medicaid Cost Sharing

Medicaid services are generally free or low-cost. Some states may require:

  • Small copayments for certain services
  • Monthly premiums (usually for higher-income enrollees)
  • Deductibles in some cases

Children, pregnant women, and people receiving long-term care services typically do not have cost-sharing requirements.

What Medicaid Covers

Medicaid provides comprehensive health coverage, including:

  • Doctor visits and hospital care
  • Prescription drugs
  • Preventive care and screenings
  • Mental health and substance use services
  • Long-term care services
  • Pregnancy and childbirth care

Coverage details vary by state. Some states offer additional benefits beyond federal requirements.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Medicaid the same as Medicare?

A: No. Medicaid is for people with limited income, while Medicare is for people 65+ or with certain disabilities.

Q: Can I have both Medicaid and Medicare?

A: Yes. People who qualify for both are called "dual eligibles."

Q: What happens if I move to another state?

A: You'll need to reapply for Medicaid in your new state, as each state runs its own program.
