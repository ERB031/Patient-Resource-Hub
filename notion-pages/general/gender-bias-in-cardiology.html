Gender Bias in Cardiology

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc

Cardiovascular disease is the #1 killer of women - but many women don’t know the signs/symptoms. Neither do their doctors - in fact, many women are sent home from the Emergency Room during a heart attack.

  • Most doctors are taught that heart attacks look like this ➡️ 
    • Until the 1990s, medical textbooks said heart disease was rare in women (not true - it affects nearly 45% of women).
    • As a result, doctors miss heart attack signs in women 50% of the time.
      • 30% of women experiencing heart attack sought medical care for their symptoms before they went to the hospital — but were told their issue wasn’t heart-related
    • Women admitted for cardiac issues are twice as likely to die in the hospital compared to men.
  • This is also how heart attacks are portrayed in the media ➡️
    • Women wait longer to go to the hospital and are less likely to think their symptoms are heart-related due to a lack of awareness about women’s heart disease.

Heart Attack Symptoms

Women

  • pain in the jaw, neck, shoulder/arm, stomach, upper back
  • indigestion/heartburn
  • nausea/vomiting
  • shortness of breath
  • palpitations
  • tingling in arms/legs
  • subtle chest tightness*
  • dizziness/fainting
  • sweating
  • extreme fatigue/sleep disturbances

(62% of women have 3+ symptoms during a heart attack)

Men

  • crushing chest pain
  • shortness of breath
  • left arm pain
  • collapse

* 2/3 of women who die from a heart attack never experience chest pain - women may experience “tightness” vs. full-blown pain because their smaller arteries are more likely to be affected

History/Timeline of Women’s Heart Disease Research

early 19th/20th centuries: heart linked to emotion, doctors believed that the people most prone to heart attacks were ambitious, successful, male - may have angry temperament to trigger

1950s: heart disease finally linked to diet, exercise, physical factors vs. emotional causes but remained a “man’s condition" (based on Ancel Keys’ research on mainly middle aged men)

1982: Multiple Risk Factor Intervention Trial (established link between cholesterol/heart disease) involved 12,866 men, 0 women

1984: more women die of heart attacks than men (previously, more men had died - but the gap narrowed as innovations/research saved more men’s lives)

1980s-1990s: research didn’t specifically exclude women, but often had requirements that made the sample group overwhelmingly male (ex: excluding women of reproductive age, etc.)

1991: Women’s Health Initiative began, Bernadine Healy (cardiologist) became the first woman to head NIH, quoted saying: “Heart disease is also a woman’s disease, not just a man’s disease in disguise.”

1995: Physicians’ Health Study (discovered that aspirin to reduced the risk of heart attack) involved 22,071 men, 0 women

2003: American Heart Association’s Go Red for Women founded

2015: Research for All Act requires NIH–funded research to use both male/female animals, cells, people
