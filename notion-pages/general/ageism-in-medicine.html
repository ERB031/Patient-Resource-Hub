Ageism in Medicine

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc
  • Providers often unconsciously associate youth with better health and resilience. As a result, patients with early-onset disease are often dismissed by medical professionals who, at first, believe that a young patient’s health concerns can’t be anything too serious due to their age. (ACS)
  • Despite advancements in the medical care provided to children with medical complexity, there is a lack of systematic approaches and guidance for patients and families transitioning from pediatric to adult health care. (PubMed)
  • Elderly patients may be treated less aggressively than young patients for certain issues (like suicidal ideation - PubMed) or overtreated for others (like UTIs - NYT)
    • Older adults may be viewed as less capable of benefiting from interventions like surgery, rehabilitation, and cancer treatments. (NIH)
  • Young adults and elderly patients often report being talked over or spoken down to, or having doctors address caregivers instead of them, even when they are cognitively intact (UCLA).
  • Both younger and older age groups are frequently excluded from clinical trials, which can result in treatments that are not properly tested for safety or efficacy in these populations:
    • Older adults are often excluded due to having multiple chronic conditions or taking multiple medications (USC)
    • Adolescents and young adults are often caught between pediatric and adult studies, and therefore underrepresented in both (NIH)
