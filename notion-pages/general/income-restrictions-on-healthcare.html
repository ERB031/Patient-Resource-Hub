Income Restrictions on Healthcare

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc
  • The US is the only developed country in the world without some form of universal healthcare (Commonwealth Fund). A patient's insurance (or lack thereof) will largely determine their access to care.¬†
    • Insurance limits which providers their clients can see (CMS).
    • Insurance may not cover things like specialty care, experimental / off-label treatments, or certain medications (FAIR Health)
    • Insurance can refuse to pay for certain items they deem ‚Äúnot medically necessary‚Äù - even if a doctor says they are. Appeal processes are lengthy and difficult to navigate, especially if you have limited time and resources (PBS)
  • In a for-profit healthcare system, the cost of basic medical care skyrockets: the US has the world‚Äôs highest medical expenses (Statisa)
    • In the US, a single¬†ambulance ride can cost hundreds of dollars - even after insurance pays their share (GoodRx)
    • At least 20 million Americans (1 in 12 adults) owe medical debt (KFF)
    • 27% of American adults forgo some form of medical care because they cannot afford it (Federal Reserve)
  • For working-class families, taking time off work to pursue healthcare can be extremely difficult.¬†Many hourly workers do not have adequate sick days and fear losing their jobs if they (or a loved one) are admitted to a hospital (CAP)
  • Some healthcare providers base their medical recommendations on a patient's perceived social class or how much they might be able to pay (Sage Journals)
¬†
