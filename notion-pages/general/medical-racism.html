Medical Racism

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc
  • The United States has a long history of racism in medicine:
    • In the late 19th century, doctors practiced surgical techniques on enslaved Black women, often without anesthetic (History)
    • In the 1930s, the US recruited hundreds of Black syphilis patients to participate in a study by promising them free medical care. In reality, the participants received placebos and diagnostic procedures while researchers observed the effects of their untreated disease for 40 years (CDC)
    • In the 1950s, Henrietta Lacks’ cells were harvested for medical research without her consent (Skloot)
    • Throughout the 20th century, hundreds of Latinx and Black women were subjected to compulsory sterilization through experimental birth control (PBS) or medically unnecessary hysterectomies, sometimes without their consent (PBS)
  • Medical students are still taught myths about Black health rooted in eugenics. For example, half of white medical trainees believe that Black people's nerve endings are less sensitive than whites’ (PNAS) 
  • Diagnostic indicators, such as pulse oximetry (used to evaluate how well someone is breathing), are dangerously inaccurate for darker-skinned patients (NEJM)
  • BIPOC have been left out of drug testing and clinical trials, compromising research findings (NIH)
  • Many doctors cannot recognize important disease features (such as blood pooling or rash) on dark skin due to a lack of diversity in medical textbooks (JAAD)
  • BIPOC have disproportionately poor health outcomes. For example, Black mothers are 243% more likely to die in childbirth than white mothers in the United States (NIH)
  • Patients of color were disproportionately affected by COVID-19, with Indigenous patients dying at higher rates than any other race in the United States (APM)
