Family Benefits

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc
When you start receiving SSDI benefits, certain members of your family may qualify for a monthly benefit of up to 50 percent of your disability benefit amount based on your work:
  • current or former spouse(s)
  • children under 18
  • adult child who became disabled before age 22
Spousal Benefits:
Benefits are payable to your spouse if they are 62 or older (unless your they collect a higher Social Security benefit based on their earnings record) or if they are caring for your child under age 16 (or an adult child who was disabled before age 22 and is entitled to benefits).
If your spouse is eligible for retirement benefits on their own record, SSA will pay that amount first. But, if the spouse’s benefit from your record is a higher amount, they will get a combination of the two benefits that equals the higher amount.
Your divorced spouse may qualify for benefits on your record, even if you have remarried, if:
  • you were married at least 10 years.
  • they are at least 62 years old
  • they are unmarried
  • they are not be eligible for an equal or higher benefit on another Social Security record
The amount of benefits payable to your divorced spouse has no effect on the amount of benefits you or your current spouse may receive.
If your spouse or ex-spouse will also receive a pension based on work not covered by Social Security, such as government or foreign work, their Social Security benefit on your record may be affected.
Child Benefits:
Your biological child, adopted child, stepchild, or dependent grandchild may also qualify to receive benefits on your record if they are unmarried and either:
  • under age 18
  • age 18-19 and a full time grade school student (grades K-12)
  • over age 18 and disabled from a disability that started before age 22
 
if the child is still a full-time student at a secondary (or elementary) school at age 18, benefits will continue until the child graduates or until two months after the child becomes age 19 (whichever is first)
Disabled Adult Child:
A disabled "adult child" who is unmarried, age 18 or older, and has a disability that meets the definition of disability for adults and started before age 22 may be eligible for benefits if a parent is deceased or starts receiving retirement or disability benefits. It is not necessary that the child ever worked because benefits are paid based on the parent's earnings record.
If the adult child is already receiving benefits on their own record, they should check to see if higher benefits may be payable on a parent's earnings record or if they are entitled to Medicare.
These benefits will generally end if the child gets married. However, some marriages (for example, to another disabled "adult child") are considered protected.
To apply for family benefits, disabled adult children should use form SSA-368-BK.
Survivor Benefits:
Your family may also be eligible to receive Social Security benefits after you die. Find more information about survivor benefits here.
