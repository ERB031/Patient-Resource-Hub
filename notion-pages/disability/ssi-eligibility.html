SSI Eligibility

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc

You are eligible for SSI if you:

  • are 65+ years old, blind⁍, or disabled⁍
  • have limited resources & income⁍
  • are a U.S. citizen or a qualified alien
  • are a resident of one of the 50 States, the District of Columbia, or Northern Mariana Islands⁍
  • are not absent from the country for more than 30 days
  • are not confined to an institution (such as a hospital or prison) at the government’s expense⁍
  • have applied for all other cash benefits you may be eligible for (such as pensions, Social Security, etc.)

If you appear to meet these requirements, your case will move on to the disability determination process.

⁍Blindness is defined as having central visual acuity for distance of 20/200 or less in your better eye with use of a correcting lens or having a visual field limitation in your better eye, such that the widest diameter of the visual field subtends an angle no greater than 20 degrees.

⁍An adult is considered disabled if they have a medically determinable physical or mental impairment which is expected to last over 12 months (or result in death) and inhibits their ability to do any substantial gainful activity. Substantial gainful activity includes full or part-time work with earnings averaging over $1,310/month for 2021.

⁍Limited resources as defined by SSA means that you have less that $2,000 in assets if you are an individual or $3,000 if you are a couple. Limited Income as defined by SSA means that you are earning less than $1,310/month after exclusions.

⁍Children of military personnel whose parent(s) are assigned to permanent duty ashore anywhere outside the United States may continue to get SSI benefits. Students who are temporarily studying abroad as part of an educational program may continue SSI payments.

⁍if you are incarcerated, you may be able to apply for prerelease benefits. More information about how going to prison affects your benefits is available here.
