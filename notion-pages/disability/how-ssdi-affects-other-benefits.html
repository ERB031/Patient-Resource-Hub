How SSDI Affects Other Benefits

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc

Medicare:

After you've been receiving (or eligible for) SSDI for 24 months⁍, you’ll be eligible for Medicare. You will get information about Medicare several months before your coverage starts.

Retirement:

If you are receiving Social Security disability benefits when you reach full retirement age, your disability benefits automatically convert to retirement benefits, but the amount remains the same.

Workers’ Compensation:

If you are receiving worker's comp⁍ payments from a federal or state workers’ compensation agencies, employer, or by insurance company on behalf of an employer, your SSDI benefit may decrease. This includes lump-sum payments.

Other Public Disability Benefits⁍:

Civil service disability benefits, state temporary disability benefits, and state or local government retirement benefits that are based on disabling medical conditions that are not job-related may decrease your SSDI benefits.

Public Benefits that do NOT affect SSDI:

  • Veterans Administration benefits
  • State and local government benefits (if Social Security taxes were deducted from your earnings)
  • Supplemental Security Income (SSI)

⁍If you have permanent kidney failure requiring regular dialysis or transplant or you have amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), you may qualify for Medicare sooner.

⁍If you receive workers’ compensation or other public disability benefits AND SSDI benefits, the total amount of these benefits cannot exceed 80% of your average current earnings before you became disabled.
