Appeals Process

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc

Each time the Social Security Administration (SSA) makes a decision about your case, it is called an "initial determination." You can appeal initial determinations about:

  • your eligibility for benefits
  • your benefit payment amount
  • whether or not you have to repay an overpayment
  • the result of an appeal

You can check the status of your appeal online.

Steps of the Appeal Process:

You must submit an appeal within 60 days of receiving written notice about a decision (SSA considers the time you receive a notice to be 5 days after the date on the notice).

  1. Reconsideration - you may request a review of SSA's initial determination about your benefits.
    1. for reconsideration of eligibility or payment amount⁍, use form SSA‑561-U2
    2. for reconsideration of disability payment cessation⁍, use form SSA-789

    ⁍If you are requesting reconsideration about a change in benefits, and submit your request for reconsideration in writing within 10 days of receiving the notice, your payments will continue until SSA makes its determination (as long as you continue to meet all other requirements)

  2. Hearing - if you disagree with the SSA's reconsideration determination, you may request a hearing from an Administrative Law Judge using form HA-501. You may also need to submit form SSA-3441 to update your disability report and form SSA-827 to authorize disclosure of information to SSA⁍.

    If you would like to submit new evidence for consideration, you must do so at least 5 days before your scheduled hearing. Subpoena requests must be made 10 days before the hearing.⁍

    1. If you do not want to appear before a judge, you may ask the judge to make a decision based on evidence in your file.
    2. If you do want to have a hearing before a judge, you must appear in person, by video teleconferencing, or by telephone (in extraordinary circumstances) at the scheduled hearing. SSA will determine the manner of appearance and provide notice of the hearing date, location, and issues to be decided 75 days before the hearing.
      • If you cannot make it to your hearing, you must contact the hearing office in writing at least 5 days before the date set for the hearing or 30 days after receiving the notice of hearing (whichever is earlier) to explain why you cannot attend. If you do not attend the scheduled hearing, you may lose your appeal rights and benefits.
      • If the distance to the hearing from your home is more than 75 miles one way, SSA may pay you for travel costs. Tell the judge as soon as possible if you need money for reasonable and necessary travel costs.
      • The judge may want you to undergo additional medical exams or tests. If this is the case, SSA will pay for and schedule the appointments for you.
      • The judge may ask other witnesses (such as medical and vocational experts) to testify at your hearing. If you would like to request certain witnesses attend the hearing, you must make this request at least 10 business days before your hearing. The judge will decide if the witness’ testimony is reasonably necessary.
      • During the hearing, the judge will explain your case and may ask you/your witnesses questions. You may also ask questions.
      • The hearings are informal, but you may request an audio recording. The judge will send you a copy of the hearing decision.

    ⁍Your Notice of Reconsideration should specify which forms are needed for an appeal. If you are unsure, you can call 800-772-1213 or your local Social Security office to clarify.

    ⁍The judge may decline to obtain or consider late submissions of evidence, objections, written statements, or subpoena requests unless you meet certain requirements listed in Social Security’s rules at 20 CFR 404.935(b) and 416.1435(b).

  3. Appeals Council - if you disagree with the judge's decision, you may request an appeal using the online appeal process or form HA-520. The Appeals Council will examine your case and will grant, deny, or dismiss your request for review.

    The Appeals Council will only review additional evidence if it is new material and there is a reasonable probability that it would change the outcome of the decision.

    If the Appeals Council grants your request for review, it will either decide your case or return it to the judge for further action, which could include another hearing and a new decision.

    • If the Appeals Council plans to issue a decision that is less than fully favorable to you, they will send you a notice of their proposed action and will allow you opportunity to respond before issuing the decision.
    • If the Appeals Council plans to issue a fully favorable decision, plans to remand your case for further proceedings, or plans to issue a decision that is partially favorable (while remanding the remaining issues for further proceedings), they will not notify you prior to taking action. They will send notice of its review with the decision or remand order.
  4. Federal Court - If the Appeals Council denies your request for review of a judge’s decision, or you disagree with the action of the Appeals Council, you may file a civil action with the U.S. District Court in your area. 

    The U.S. District Court will review the evidence and the final Agency decision. They may send the case back to the Agency, order a judge to hold a new hearing, direct the agency to award benefits, or dismiss the case.
