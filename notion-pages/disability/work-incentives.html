Work Incentives

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc
Work incentive employment supports benefit recipients going to work by minimizing the risk of losing their benefits. There are a variety of work incentive programs available to meet different people's needs.
If you need help navigating work incentives, most Social Security offices have a Work Incentive Liaison (WIL) or Area Work Incentives Coordinator (AWIC) who can help you. You may also use a Benefits Planning Query (BPQ) to understand how working will affect your disability cash benefits, health insurance, scheduled continuing disability reviews, etc. You can request a BPQ at your local office or by calling 1-800-772-1213 (TTY/TDD: 1-800-325-0778)
 

SSI Work Incentives:

Earned Income Exclusion
SSA does not count the first $65 of the earnings you receive in a month, plus one-half of the remaining earnings. (This means that they count less than one-half of your earnings when calculating your SSI payment amount.) This exclusion is applied in addition to the $20 general income exclusion. (The $20 general income exclusion is first applied to any unearned income that you may receive.)
Student Earned Income Exclusion
The Student Earned Income Exclusion provision allows SSI Recipients who are under age 22 and regularly attending school to exclude earnings from up to $1,930/month from their income. (The maximum yearly exclusion is $7,550.)
Regularly attending school means you are taking one or more courses & attend classes:
  • in a college/university for at least 8hr/week under a semester or quarter system
  • in a training course to prepare for employment for at least 12hr/week (15hr/week if the course involves shop practice)
  • homeschooled (grades 7-12) for at least 12hr/week & in accordance with the home school law of the state
  • in a course(s) given by a school (grades 7–12), college, university, or government agency
  • has a home visitor/tutor from school who directs the studying/training
(students may still be eligible if they attend study for less time than indicated above for reasons beyond their control, such as illness)
Benefit Continuation for People Who Work (Section 1619)
Section 1619(a) provides that some people working at or above the SGA level may be able to continue to receive cash payments and Medicaid benefits. To qualify, you must have been eligible for an SSI payment for at least one month before you begin working at the SGA level; you must still be disabled; and you must meet all other eligibility rules (including the income and resource tests.)
Section 1619(b) provides that, in most cases, even if your earned income is too high to permit an SSI cash benefit, you may still be eligible for Medicaid as long as you need Medicaid in order to work and your earnings are under a “threshold” amount. You remain eligible for SSI and may begin receiving cash benefits again without a new SSI application if your earnings drop.
Under Section 1619, you may also be eligible for a regular SSI benefit for up to 2 months after entering a medical institution.

SSDI & SSI Work Incentives:

Impairment–Related Work Expenses (IRWE)
If your disabling impairment means that you have to pay for certain items in order to work, SSA may be able to deduct the costs of these items from the amount of earnings they use to calculate your SSI/SSDI benefit.
Items that may be considered Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWEs):
  • medicine
  • medical supplies (including disposable items such as bandages and syringes)
  • medical devices
  • service animals
  • medical services (doctors visits, attendant care services that prepare you for work, attend to you during work, or getting you to and from work)
  • transportation
  • modifications to your home, car, or van that allow you to work
The expenses cannot be reimbursed, and must be related to your disability(ies) and needed in order for you to work. Item or services that are also used for daily living are still included. (For example, the cost of a wheelchair usually can be deducted even though it is used for both daily living and work.)
Expedited Reinstatement of Benefits
Expedited Reinstatement of Benefits (EXR) provides a safety net for people who successfully return to work and lose their benefits. If your cash payment ended because of your earnings, and you stop work within 5 years of when your benefits ended due to your medical condition, you may be able to receive temporary cash benefits and Medicaid for up to 6 months while SSA conducts a medical review to determine whether your benefits will be fully reinstated. (SSA will need updated medical information for the DDS to make their determination.)
Continuation of Benefits after Medical Cessation (Section 301)
If SSA finds that you are no longer disabled due to medical improvement or that you do meet the adult requirements for disability, your benefit payments will stop, unless you are participating in an appropriate program of vocational rehabilitation (VR) or similar services. To qualify for continued payments, you must meet these requirements:
  • you have been participating in an appropriate program of the VR since before your disability ended
  • SSA finds evidence that your continued participation in the program will increase the likelihood of your permanent removal from the disability benefit rolls
Participation in the following programs may deem you eligible for this continuation:
  • an individualized education program (IEP) for people ages 18-21
  • Ticket To Work
  • a Vocational Rehabilitation Agency using an individualized plan for employment (IPE)
  • support services using an individualized written employment plan
  • Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS)
Plan to Achieve Self Support (PASS)
A PASS is a written plan of action for getting a particular kind of job or starting a business. You can start a PASS if:
  • you would be eligible for SSI based on disability if not for your income and/or assests
  • you are already eligible for SSI but your income reduces the amount of SSI you receive
  • using a PASS will eventually help you to reduce/eliminate benefits you receive from SSI, Social Security, or both.
Your PASS should identify:
  • your work goal (a job or business)
  • steps you will take, things you will need to achieve your work goal (such as education, transportation, assistive technology, etc.)
  • money you will use to pay for these things (any income other than SSI payments)
  • a timeline for achieving your goal
If SSA approves your PASS, the money you spend on your plan will not be counted when determining your eligibility for SSI.
To create your PASS, use form SSA-545-BK. You may receive help from a friend, family member, or vocational counselor. SSA Publication No. 05-11017 has more information about the PASS program and how to use it.

SSDI Work Incentives:

Ticket to Work
Benefit recipients who are 18-64 and interested in returning to work can receive free benefits counseling from Work Incentive Planning and Assistance (WIPA) projects and/or free employment services from an employment network using Ticket to Work. This program was created to help you find a good job, a good career and a better self-supporting future. While you are participating in the Ticket to Work program, SSA will not conduct medical reviews of your case.
Ticket to Work also created Protection & Advocacy for Beneficiaries of Social Security (PABSS) organizations who can assist you with understanding employment rights, reasonable accommodations, and filing complaints about an employment network or your State Vocational Rehabilitation Agency.
 
WIPA projects are community-based organizations that will help you understand the employment supports that are available to you, how work will affect your benefits, and enable you to make informed choices about work.
Trial Work Period (TWP)
The Trial Work Period allows you to test your ability to work for at least 9 months. During your trial work period, you’ll receive your full SSDI (this program does not apply to SSI) benefits (regardless of how much you’re earning) as long as you report your work and continue to have a disability.
In 2021, any month your total earnings are over $940 counts as a trial work month. Your trial work period continues until you have used nine trial work months within a 60-month period.
 
these months do not have to be sequential to count. You only get 9 trial work months in a 60-month period no matter how spread out they are.
Extended Period of Eligibility
After your trial work period ends, you have 36 months during which you can work and still receive benefits for any month in which your earnings aren’t “substantial.” In 2021, earnings over $1,310 are considered substantial. You do not need to file a new application or disability decision during this period.
$2,190 if you are blind
Continuation of Medicare
If your SSDI benefits stop because of your earnings, but you’re still disabled, your free Medicare Part A coverage will continue for at least 93 months after the 9 month trial work period.
After that, you can buy Medicare Part A coverage by paying a monthly premium. If you have Medicare Part B coverage, you must continue to pay the premium. If you want to end your Part B coverage, you must request it in writing.

This program was created to help you find a good job, a good career and a better self-supporting future. While you are participating in the Ticket to Work program, SSA will not conduct medical reviews of your case.
Ticket to Work also created Protection & Advocacy for Beneficiaries of Social Security (PABSS) organizations who can assist you with understanding employment rights, reasonable accommodations, and filing complaints about an employment network or your State Vocational Rehabilitation Agency.
 
WIPA projects are community-based organizations that will help you understand the employment supports that are available to you, how work will affect your benefits, and enable you to make informed choices about work.
Trial Work Period (TWP)
The Trial Work Period allows you to test your ability to work for at least 9 months. During your trial work period, you’ll receive your full SSDI (this program does not apply to SSI) benefits (regardless of how much you’re earning) as long as you report your work and continue to have a disability.
In 2021, any month your total earnings are over $940 counts as a trial work month. Your trial work period continues until you have used nine trial work months within a 60-month period.
 
these months do not have to be sequential to count. You only get 9 trial work months in a 60-month period no matter how spread out they are.
Extended Period of Eligibility
After your trial work period ends, you have 36 months during which you can work and still receive benefits for any month in which your earnings aren’t “substantial.” In 2021, earnings over $1,310 are considered substantial. You do not need to file a new application or disability decision during this period.
$2,190 if you are blind
Continuation of Medicare
If your SSDI benefits stop because of your earnings, but you’re still disabled, your free Medicare Part A coverage will continue for at least 93 months after the 9 month trial work period.
After that, you can buy Medicare Part A coverage by paying a monthly premium. If you have Medicare Part B coverage, you must continue to pay the premium. If you want to end your Part B coverage, you must request it in writing.
 
Work incentive employment supports benefit recipients going to work by minimizing the risk of losing their benefits. There are a variety of work incentive programs available to meet different people's needs.
If you need help navigating work incentives, most Social Security offices have a Work Incentive Liaison (WIL) or Area Work Incentives Coordinator (AWIC) who can help you. You may also use a Benefits Planning Query (BPQ) to understand how working will affect your disability cash benefits, health insurance, scheduled continuing disability reviews, etc. You can request a BPQ at your local office or by calling 1-800-772-1213 (TTY/TDD: 1-800-325-0778)
 

SSI Work Incentives:

Earned Income Exclusion
SSA does not count the first $65 of the earnings you receive in a month, plus one-half of the remaining earnings. (This means that they count less than one-half of your earnings when calculating your SSI payment amount.) This exclusion is applied in addition to the $20 general income exclusion. (The $20 general income exclusion is first applied to any unearned income that you may receive.)
Student Earned Income Exclusion
The Student Earned Income Exclusion provision allows SSI Recipients who are under age 22 and regularly attending school to exclude earnings from up to $1,930/month from their income. (The maximum yearly exclusion is $7,550.)
Regularly attending school means you are taking one or more courses & attend classes:
  • in a college/university for at least 8hr/week under a semester or quarter system
  • in a training course to prepare for employment for at least 12hr/week (15hr/week if the course involves shop practice)
  • homeschooled (grades 7-12) for at least 12hr/week & in accordance with the home school law of the state
  • in a course(s) given by a school (grades 7–12), college, university, or government agency
  • has a home visitor/tutor from school who directs the studying/training
(students may still be eligible if they attend study for less time than indicated above for reasons beyond their control, such as illness)
Benefit Continuation for People Who Work (Section 1619)
Section 1619(a) provides that some people working at or above the SGA level may be able to continue to receive cash payments and Medicaid benefits. To qualify, you must have been eligible for an SSI payment for at least one month before you begin working at the SGA level; you must still be disabled; and you must meet all other eligibility rules (including the income and resource tests.)
Section 1619(b) provides that, in most cases, even if your earned income is too high to permit an SSI cash benefit, you may still be eligible for Medicaid as long as you need Medicaid in order to work and your earnings are under a “threshold” amount. You remain eligible for SSI and may begin receiving cash benefits again without a new SSI application if your earnings drop.
Under Section 1619, you may also be eligible for a regular SSI benefit for up to 2 months after entering a medical institution.

SSDI & SSI Work Incentives:

Impairment–Related Work Expenses (IRWE)
If your disabling impairment means that you have to pay for certain items in order to work, SSA may be able to deduct the costs of these items from the amount of earnings they use to calculate your SSI/SSDI benefit.
Items that may be considered Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWEs):
  • medicine
  • medical supplies (including disposable items such as bandages and syringes)
  • medical devices
  • service animals
  • medical services (doctors visits, attendant care services that prepare you for work, attend to you during work, or getting you to and from work)
  • transportation
  • modifications to your home, car, or van that allow you to work
The expenses cannot be reimbursed, and must be related to your disability(ies) and needed in order for you to work. Item or services that are also used for daily living are still included. (For example, the cost of a wheelchair usually can be deducted even though it is used for both daily living and work.)
Expedited Reinstatement of Benefits
Expedited Reinstatement of Benefits (EXR) provides a safety net for people who successfully return to work and lose their benefits. If your cash payment ended because of your earnings, and you stop work within 5 years of when your benefits ended due to your medical condition, you may be able to receive temporary cash benefits and Medicaid for up to 6 months while SSA conducts a medical review to determine whether your benefits will be fully reinstated. (SSA will need updated medical information for the DDS to make their determination.)
Continuation of Benefits after Medical Cessation (Section 301)
If SSA finds that you are no longer disabled due to medical improvement or that you do meet the adult requirements for disability, your benefit payments will stop, unless you are participating in an appropriate program of vocational rehabilitation (VR) or similar services. To qualify for continued payments, you must meet these requirements:
  • you have been participating in an appropriate program of the VR since before your disability ended
  • SSA finds evidence that your continued participation in the program will increase the likelihood of your permanent removal from the disability benefit rolls
Participation in the following programs may deem you eligible for this continuation:
  • an individualized education program (IEP) for people ages 18-21
  • Ticket To Work
  • a Vocational Rehabilitation Agency using an individualized plan for employment (IPE)
  • support services using an individualized written employment plan
  • Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS)
Plan to Achieve Self Support (PASS)
A PASS is a written plan of action for getting a particular kind of job or starting a business. You can start a PASS if:
  • you would be eligible for SSI based on disability if not for your income and/or assests
  • you are already eligible for SSI but your income reduces the amount of SSI you receive
  • using a PASS will eventually help you to reduce/eliminate benefits you receive from SSI, Social Security, or both.
Your PASS should identify:
  • your work goal (a job or business)
  • steps you will take, things you will need to achieve your work goal (such as education, transportation, assistive technology, etc.)
  • money you will use to pay for these things (any income other than SSI payments)
  • a timeline for achieving your goal
If SSA approves your PASS, the money you spend on your plan will not be counted when determining your eligibility for SSI.
To create your PASS, use form SSA-545-BK. You may receive help from a friend, family member, or vocational counselor. SSA Publication No. 05-11017 has more information about the PASS program and how to use it.

  • you have been participating in an appropriate program of the VR since before your disability ended
  • SSA finds evidence that your continued participation in the program will increase the likelihood of your permanent removal from the disability benefit rolls
Participation in the following programs may deem you eligible for this continuation:
  • an individualized education program (IEP) for people ages 18-21
  • Ticket To Work
  • a Vocational Rehabilitation Agency using an individualized plan for employment (IPE)
  • support services using an individualized written employment plan
  • Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS)
Plan to Achieve Self Support (PASS)
A PASS is a written plan of action for getting a particular kind of job or starting a business. You can start a PASS if:
  • you would be eligible for SSI based on disability if not for your income and/or assests
  • you are already eligible for SSI but your income reduces the amount of SSI you receive
  • using a PASS will eventually help you to reduce/eliminate benefits you receive from SSI, Social Security, or both.
Your PASS should identify:
  • your work goal (a job or business)
  • steps you will take, things you will need to achieve your work goal (such as education, transportation, assistive technology, etc.)
  • money you will use to pay for these things (any income other than SSI payments)
  • a timeline for achieving your goal
If SSA approves your PASS, the money you spend on your plan will not be counted when determining your eligibility for SSI.
To create your PASS, use form SSA-545-BK. You may receive help from a friend, family member, or vocational counselor. SSA Publication No. 05-11017 has more information about the PASS program and how to use it.

SSDI Work Incentives:

Ticket to Work
Benefit recipients who are 18-64 and interested in returning to work can receive free benefits counseling from Work Incentive Planning and Assistance (WIPA) projects and/or free employment services from an employment network using Ticket to Work. This program was created to help you find a good job, a good career and a better self-supporting future. While you are participating in the Ticket to Work program, SSA will not conduct medical reviews of your case.
Ticket to Work also created Protection & Advocacy for Beneficiaries of Social Security (PABSS) organizations who can assist you with understanding employment rights, reasonable accommodations, and filing complaints about an employment network or your State Vocational Rehabilitation Agency.
 
WIPA projects are community-based organizations that will help you understand the employment supports that are available to you, how work will affect your benefits, and enable you to make informed choices about work.
Trial Work Period (TWP)
The Trial Work Period allows you to test your ability to work for at least 9 months. During your trial work period, you’ll receive your full SSDI (this program does not apply to SSI) benefits (regardless of how much you’re earning) as long as you report your work and continue to have a disability.
In 2021, any month your total earnings are over $940 counts as a trial work month. Your trial work period continues until you have used nine trial work months within a 60-month period.
 
these months do not have to be sequential to count. You only get 9 trial work months in a 60-month period no matter how spread out they are.
Extended Period of Eligibility
After your trial work period ends, you have 36 months during which you can work and still receive benefits for any month in which your earnings aren’t “substantial.” In 2021, earnings over $1,310 are considered substantial. You do not need to file a new application or disability decision during this period.

Continuation of Medicare
If your SSDI benefits stop because of your earnings, but you’re still disabled, your free Medicare Part A coverage will continue for at least 93 months after the 9 month trial work period.
After that, you can buy Medicare Part A coverage by paying a monthly premium. If you have Medicare Part B coverage, you must continue to pay the premium. If you want to end your Part B coverage, you must request it in writing.
