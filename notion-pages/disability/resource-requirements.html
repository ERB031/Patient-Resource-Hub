Resource Requirements

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc
Resources are things that you own, including:
  • cash
  • bank accounts, stocks, & bonds
  • land
  • life insurance
  • anything else you own which could be changed to cash and used for food or shelter
  • personal property
  • vehicles
  • deemed resources
  • trusts
SSI has limits to how many resources you can own while receiving benefits. If the value of your resources that SSA counts is over the allowable limit ($2,000 for an individual or $3,000 for a couple for SSI) at the beginning of the month, you cannot receive SSI for that month. If you decide to sell the excess resources for what they are worth, you may receive SSI beginning the month after you sell the excess resources.
SSDI is not impacted by resources.
 
Resources that do not count when calculating benefits:
  • the home that you live in and the land that it is on
  • one vehicle (if you or a member of your household use it for transportation)
  • household goods and personal effects
  • life insurance worth $1,500 or less
  • burial spaces for you or your immediate family and $1,500 or less of burial funds for you or your spouse
  • property you use in a trade or business (property needed for self support)
  • money or property you have set aside under a Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS)
  • up to $100,000 of funds in an ABLE Account
  • support and maintenance assistance, home energy assistance, and disaster relief that does not count as income
  • health flexible spending arrangements (FSAs)
  • dedicated accounts for disabled or blind children
  • money saved in an Individual Development Account (IDA)
  • Special Needs Trusts under Section 1917(d)(4)(a)
  • Pooled Trusts under Section 1917(d)(4)(c)
 
The following do not count as a resource for a certain time period:
  • retroactive benefits for up to 9 months after you receive them (including payments received in installments)
  • grants, scholarships, fellowships, or gifts set aside to pay educational expenses for 9 months after receipt
  • cash received for medical or social services that does not count as income for 1 month after receipt
  • State or local relocation assistance payments for 12 months after receipt
  • crime victim's assistance for 9 months after receipt
  • earned income tax credit payments for 9 months after receipt
  • cash received for the purpose of replacing an excluded resource (for example, a house) that is lost, damaged, or stolen for 9 months after receipt
  • Federal tax refunds and advanced tax credits received on or after January 1, 2010 are not counted for 12 months
 
If you, your spouse, or a co–owner give away a resource or sell it for less than it is worth, you may be ineligible for certain benefits for up to 36 months. How long you are ineligible benefits depends on the value of the resource you transferred.
If you sell a resource for what it is worth, the 36–month ineligibility period does not apply. But, the money you receive from the sale may make you ineligible if it puts you over the resource limit. More information about transferring & selling resources.
 
Deemed resources are a portion of the resources of a spouse, parent, parent’s spouse, sponsor of an alien, or sponsor’s spouse as belonging to the person who applies for SSI.
Revocable trusts established on or after January 1, 2000 will count as a resource. In the case of an irrevocable trust, if there are any circumstances under which payment could be made to you (for your benefit), that portion of the trust is your resource.
