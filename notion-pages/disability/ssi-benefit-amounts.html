SSI Benefit Amounts

This page mirrors our Notion doc so you can browse without leaving the site.

View original Notion doc

Your SSI benefit is calculated by deducting your countable income from the maximum Federal Benefit Amount ($794 for an individual and $1,191 for a couple⁍).

If you are living in someone else's household and not paying for your own food/living expenses, your benefit will be decreased by one-third ($529.34 for an individual and $794 for a couple). If you are living in a Medicaid institution, your benefit will decrease to $30. For more information, see the section on living arrangements.

⁍Each January, SSI & SSDI benefits will increase if the cost of living has gone up

Some states supplement the Federal SSI benefit with additional payments.

The following states do not provide a supplement:

Arizona, Mississippi, North Dakota, West Virginia, Northern Mariana Islands

The following states have a state supplement that is administered by Social Security:

California, Delaware*, District of Columbia*, Hawaii, Iowa*, Michigan, Montana, Nevada, New Jersey, Pennsylvania*, Rhode Island*, Vermont

The following States pay and administer their own supplemental payments:

Alabama, Alaska, Arkansas, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware*, District of Columbia*, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa*, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Mexico, New York, North Carolina, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Pennsylvania*, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin, Wyoming

(States marked with a * are dual administration states. Social Security administers some categories of State supplement payments, while the State administers others.)
