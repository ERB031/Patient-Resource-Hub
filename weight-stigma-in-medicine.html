Weight Stigma in Medicine

  • Patients frequently report that their health concerns are blamed on their weight without further investigation, or that treatment options are contingent on their weight loss (BMC)
    • BMI is still commonly used to measure body composition (and sometimes serves as a prerequisite for certain medical treatments), even though research shows it is not an accurate health indicator (NIH)
  • The medical field is the second most common place to experience weight bias (PMC)
    • Doctors spend less time in appointments with their higher-weight patients and are more likely to view them as “non-compliant” (NIH)
    • Over half of higher-weight patients report receiving inappropriate comments about their weight from their doctors (NIH)
    • Not all medical facilities are equipped to care for larger patients: ill-fitting gowns, too-small blood pressure cuffs, exam and x-ray tables that cannot support higher weights, and small waiting room chairs all create barriers to healthcare for people with larger bodies (Wiley)
  • Patients who felt stigmatized about their weight are less likely to trust their primary care doctor or may avoid going to the doctor altogether (Wiley, Cambridge)
