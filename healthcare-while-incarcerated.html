Healthcare While Incarcerated

  • Correctional institutions are required to provide basic care to inmates, but often cut corners to save money (especially when essential services are contracted out to private companies):
    • Food served to inmates is often not adequate to maintain good health. Prisoners across the country report meager portions, skipped meals, and rotting food being served in correctional facilities. (Marshall Project)
    • Soap, menstrual products, and PPE are not readily available to inmates without commissary money. (PHR)
    • Medication is not always distributed on-time, even when inconsistent dosing can have detrimental health consequences (Spokesman)
    • Healthcare providers are short-staffed, have inadequate training, and may split their time between different facilities (Pew, Marshall Project)
    • Mental healthcare in prison is largely served in solitary confinement, which is known to induce detrimental psychological effects (Solitary Watch)
  • Nearly half of prisoners have a chronic health condition (DOJ). However, many incarcerated people report that their health concerns are not taken seriously by corrections officers and that they are denied appointments to see medical staff. (Marshall Project)
  • Most facilities do not offer substance abuse treatment, leaving hundreds of patients to detox in cells, alone. (PPI)
  • Fewer than 5% of prisons have a hospice care program, despite 200,000 people sentenced to die behind bars (Humane Prison Hospice Project)
  • Many other components of prison healthcare are dangerous: from transport vehicles, to restraint chairs, to giving birth while shackled.
